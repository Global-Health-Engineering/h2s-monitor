# H2S Monitor — PCB Design Review

**Reviewed file:** `hardware/h2s-pcb/H2S_Sensor.kicad_sch`
**Target sensor:** SUCH H2S, 0–2000 ppm range
- Sensitivity: 85 ± 25 nA/ppm
- Resolution: 3 ppm
- Max load concentration: 4000 ppm
- Bias: 0 V, R_load: 5–30 Ω
- Response time: < 30 s
**Form factor:** hand-held, battery-operated, on-board OLED display
**Battery:** 1(2?)× 18650 Li-ion cell (3.0–4.2 V, ≈3000 mAh)

---

## 1. What's good (do not change)

- **U4 LMP91002** is the right AFE. It integrates the control amp, TIA, and bias DAC in one chip; sensor pins go directly to it (no external R_gain or R_load needed). 0 V bias and 10 Ω internal R_load are software-selectable and meet the spec exactly.
- **Sensor electrical match for 0–2000 ppm**: with the LMP91002 TIA programmed to **7 kΩ**, full-scale (2000 ppm × 85 nA/ppm = 170 µA) → 1.19 V, max-load (4000 ppm × 85 nA/ppm = 340 µA) → 2.38 V. Both fit comfortably under a 3.3 V rail with headroom for noise.
  - **Firmware note:** hard-set TIA = 7 kΩ at boot. Do **not** auto-range. Gain switching on a high-impedance electrochemical cell causes transient currents that take seconds to settle and corrupt readings.
- **U3 XIAO ESP32-C6** is a fine MCU choice: USB-C, USB-Serial-JTAG, Wi-Fi/BLE for data export, all on the module.
- **DS3232M RTC + CR2032 backup** is appropriate for time-stamped logging; the DS3232M's TCXO is also accurate enough that you can use its temperature register (±3 °C) as a coarse temperature-compensation source for the cell.
- **Pump driver** (Q2 AO3400 + D1 flyback + R10/R11) is correct for a low-side PWM pump.
- **Single I²C bus** for AFE + RTC + OLED + (additions below) is acceptable; just sequence accesses in firmware so an OLED refresh doesn't block an AFE sample.

---

## 2. Issues to fix before fabrication

Issues are ordered by priority.

### 2.1 Power architecture — switch to a proper 3.3 V LDO for the AFE

**Problem.** Now, +3.3 V is generated by the XIAO ESP32-C6's onboard LDO from the `SYS` rail. That LDO has a dropout of a few hundred mV. With an 18650 cell discharging from 4.2 V to 3.0 V, the rail will start sagging once VBAT drops below ~3.5 V — i.e., for the entire bottom third of the battery's capacity.

**Why this matters.** The LMP91002's reference and bias voltages scale with VDD. A drooping rail directly translates to drift in the measured ppm value, on top of any real H2S concentration changes. For a 0–2000 ppm instrument with 3 ppm resolution, the rail must be rock-solid.

**Action.**
- Add a dedicated low-Iq LDO between `SYS` and a new rail `+3V3_AFE`, powering only the LMP91002, the external ADC (item 2.2), and the voltage reference (item 2.3).
- Suggested part: **TI TPS7A0233** (200 mA, 25 nA quiescent, 200 mV dropout) or **TLV75733P** (1 A, low noise).
- Keep the XIAO's onboard LDO for digital +3V3 (OLED, MCU, SD).
- This separation also keeps OLED refresh transients off the analog rail.

### 2.2 ADC resolution and reference — use an external ΔΣ ADC

**Problem.** With TIA = 7 kΩ, 3 ppm of H2S corresponds to ≈1.78 mV at the LMP91002 output. The ESP32-C6's 12-bit ADC has a step of ≈0.8 mV (3.3 V / 4096), but its effective number of bits is only ~10 in practice (≈3.2 mV / LSB) due to nonlinearity and noise. That puts 3 ppm at less than 1 effective LSB — you will **not** resolve the spec.

**Why this matters.** The MCU ADC is the single weakest link in the measurement chain. The whole design effort is wasted if the digitiser is below the analog noise floor.

**Action.**
- Add an external **16-bit ΔΣ ADC on I²C**: **TI ADS1115** (4 channels, 860 SPS, programmable gain, internal reference). Step ≈ 62.5 µV at PGA = 1 → 3 ppm = ≈28 LSB. Plenty of margin.
- Wire one input to `V_SENSE` (LMP91002 VOUT), one to a divided VBAT for redundant battery monitoring, leave two spare for future use (e.g., temperature, second sensor).
- Power it from `+3V3_AFE` (item 2.1).
- Alternative: **MCP3421** (18-bit, single channel, smaller, also I²C) if you need fewer pins / lower cost.

### 2.3 Voltage reference — add an external precision reference

**Problem.** Even with a clean LDO, an LDO is not a precision reference (1–2 % initial accuracy, ≥50 ppm/°C drift). You'll also calibrate each unit against a known H2S source, but the calibration will drift with the rail.

**Why this matters.** The ADS1115 has an internal reference (2.048 V, 0.05 %, 25 ppm/°C) that's used when its PGA is set to ±2.048 V. **If you choose the ADS1115, you do not need a separate Vref chip** — its internal one is good enough.

**Action.**
- If using ADS1115: configure PGA = ±2.048 V (matches the 0–2.38 V max-load swing nicely after a small voltage divider, or PGA = ±4.096 V to take it directly).
- If using the ESP32-C6 ADC instead: add an **LM4040-2.5** shunt reference on a spare ADC channel and use it as a software-applied scale-correction reference (ratiometric reading).

### 2.4 Battery: 18650-specific changes

The schematic currently uses a generic LiPo connector (`J2`, DF13-02) and a **BQ25185** charger (1 A, linear). Switching to a 1× 18650 cell changes several things:

#### 2.4.1 Charger — keep BQ25185, but plan for slow charge

**Problem.** A 1 A linear charger will charge a 3000 mAh 18650 in ≈3.5 h (0.33 C). That's acceptable, but linear chargers dissipate (VBUS − VBAT) × ICHG as heat — at 5 V in / 3.7 V cell / 1 A = 1.3 W in the IC. The BQ25185 is a small DFN; it will throttle on thermal limit.

**Action.**
- **Option A (cheap, simple, slower):** keep BQ25185, set ICHG = 500 mA (cuts heat in half, ~6 h charge). Add thermal vias under the IC.
- **Option B (faster, more board work):** replace with a switching charger — **BQ25895** (5 A, I²C-controlled) or **MP2632B** (combined charger + boost). Recommended if charge time matters for users in the field.

#### 2.4.2 Holder, protection, fusing

**Problem.** 18650 cells are user-replaceable; users *will* insert them backwards, short the contacts with keys, or use unprotected cells.

**Action.**
- Use a **Keystone 1042** or **Keystone 1048** through-hole 18650 holder, oriented so the user can see the polarity marking on the PCB silkscreen.
- Add a **resettable PTC fuse** (e.g., 2 A hold / 4 A trip, 0805) in series with the cell.
- Add an **ideal-diode P-FET** for reverse polarity protection (e.g., **SiA923EDJ** or **DMP3098L** + a small reference). Even with a keyed holder, a backwards cell or a damaged contact can short the charger input.
- Recommend (in documentation) **protected 18650 cells** that include their own over-discharge / over-current PCB. If users will be supplying their own cells, you cannot rely on this — design assuming an unprotected cell and add over-discharge protection on-board (the fuel gauge in 2.4.3 helps, plus a software cutoff at 3.0 V).

#### 2.4.3 Fuel gauge — add MAX17048

**Problem.** The current `VBAT_SENSE` divider is **R8 = 1 MΩ / R9 = 510 kΩ** → source impedance ≈339 kΩ. The ESP32-C6 ADC wants ≤100 kΩ; at this impedance you get attenuation, nonlinearity, and slow settling. And a voltage divider gives only a crude SoC estimate (Li-ion voltage curves are flat over most of the discharge).

**Why this matters.** A hand-held instrument needs a trustworthy "battery low" indicator. Field users will not trust a percentage that jumps from 80 % to 20 %.

**Action.**
- Add **Maxim MAX17048G+T10** (I²C ModelGauge, no sense resistor, uses a Li-ion model). It reads VBAT directly, reports SoC %, and has an `ALRT` interrupt pin you can wire to a spare ESP32 GPIO.
- Delete `R8` and `R9`. Read battery voltage from the MAX17048 over I²C instead of via ADC.

### 2.5 Power-on / power-off — add a soft power switch

**Problem.** Currently the device is powered whenever a battery is inserted or USB is connected. Quiescent draw is the sum of (XIAO ESP32-C6 sleep current + BQ25185 quiescent + LMP91002 quiescent). For a hand-held with a user-replaceable cell, this means the cell drains in storage.

**Action.**
- Add a **soft latching power switch**: **TI TPS22916** (load switch) + a momentary push-button + a few resistors, or use a single-chip latch like **STMicro STM6601**.
- The button toggles `SYS` → `+3V3_AFE` and the XIAO's enable. The MCU can also turn itself off by de-asserting the latch (long-press = shutdown).
- Wake/charge: USB insertion should bypass the latch and wake the MCU so the user can see charging status.

### 2.6 Charging status — wire up BQ25185 STAT/PG

**Problem.** From the schematic labels alone, it's not clear whether the BQ25185 `STAT` (charge status, open-drain) and `PG` (power-good) pins are wired to MCU GPIOs.

**Action.**
- **Verify** these are connected to two ESP32-C6 GPIOs with appropriate pull-ups.
- The MCU should display "Charging" / "Full" / "USB connected" on the OLED based on these signals. This is the primary way users will know the device is charging without opening it.
- If you'd rather skip the GPIOs: route `STAT` to a small surface-mount LED through a current-limit resistor on the case-visible side of the board.

### 2.7 LMP91002 MENB — wire to MCU GPIO

**Problem.** The LMP91002 has a `MENB` (module enable, active-low) pin that puts the chip into a 0.6 µA standby state. From the labels it's not clear this is connected to a GPIO.

**Why this matters.** For a hand-held with intermittent measurements, the AFE should be in standby between samples. With a 0–2000 ppm cell and < 30 s response time, you can afford to wake the AFE every few seconds, sample, and put it back to sleep — saving most of its ~10 µA active current.

**Action.**
- **Verify** `MENB` is on a dedicated MCU GPIO. If it's tied to GND on the schematic (always-on), break that connection and route to a GPIO.

### 2.8 User interface — add a second button

**Problem.** `SW1` is the only button. A hand-held instrument with menu navigation, range selection, calibration trigger, data-logging start/stop, and shutdown needs more than one input.

**Action.**
- Add at least one more tactile button (menu / select / back). Two is the minimum; three gives a comfortable UI (up / down / select).
- Alternative: an **EC11 rotary encoder + push** gives all three functions in one part.
- Whatever you pick, all buttons must be reachable with one hand while holding the device — plan the enclosure now.

### 2.9 Temperature compensation — improve

**Problem.** Electrochemical H2S cells drift ≈ 0.5 %/°C in sensitivity and have a temperature-dependent zero offset. Spec'd operating range is −30 °C to +50 °C — that's an 80 °C swing, which can be ≈40 % drift uncompensated.

**Action.**
- **Minimum:** read the DS3232M's internal temperature register (±3 °C accuracy). Apply a per-degree correction in firmware; document the table or curve in firmware comments.
- **Better:** add a dedicated T/RH sensor — **Sensirion SHT41** (I²C, ±0.2 °C, ±1.8 %RH, 6-pin DFN). Same I²C bus. RH also lets you flag readings outside the cell's 15–90 %RH operating window.
- The ADS1115 (item 2.2) has a spare channel — you could also wire a thermistor closer to the sensor cell, which is more representative than a chip-temperature reading from elsewhere on the board.

## 4. Items to verify on the existing schematic (no parts to add, just check)

- [ ] **BQ25185 STAT and PG** are routed to MCU GPIOs (or to LEDs).
- [ ] **LMP91002 MENB** is routed to a MCU GPIO, not tied to GND.
- [ ] **R7 (10 kΩ)** is acting as the DS3232M `INT/SQW` pull-up — confirm.
- [ ] **I²C pull-ups (R14/R15, 4.7 kΩ)**: with the additions above the bus will have 5–6 devices. 4.7 kΩ is fine at 100 kHz; verify if you want 400 kHz Fast-mode.
- [ ] **No power switch in series with battery** — see 2.5.
- [ ] **Q1 PMOS gate**: confirm its gate is actively driven, not just pulled high. Otherwise it's always on and not really a load switch.
- [ ] **Battery polarity** on `J2` matches the 18650 holder pin-out you'll specify.

## 5. Order to tackle the changes

In limited time, address in this order:

1. **2.4 (battery: holder + fuse + reverse protection + fuel gauge)** — biggest functional impact for a hand-held with replaceable 18650 cells.
2. **2.1 (LDO) + 2.2 (ADS1115)** — required to actually meet 3 ppm resolution.
3. **2.6 + 2.7 (verify BQ25185 STAT/PG and LMP91002 MENB are on GPIOs)** — likely just a wiring fix on the existing schematic; MENB also unlocks low-power standby.
4. **2.5 (soft power switch)** — quality-of-life, but important for storage/runtime with user-replaceable cells.
5. **2.8 (second button) + 2.9 (temperature compensation)** — UX and accuracy refinements.
